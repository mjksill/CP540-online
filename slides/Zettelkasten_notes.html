Recording information

Your mind is for having ideas, not holding them.

So why should you take notes?

One obvious reason is to record information, and this can be thoughts, ideas, methods of doing things, etc.

Just think of all the times that you have seen something interesting, like either a potentially useful piece of data or a nice explanation of a complicated concept, or an interesting YouTube video. You perhaps leave the tab on your browser open or you might write down a note on a piece of paper (or even in a notebook)

A big problem is that while you are working on something, such as a report or on some calculation, you might recall that you had come across some that would be useful interesting fact or piece of information

A lot of times you will find yourself writing a fact in a report and then trying to remember where you got that information in order to provide a reference. This might be a correlation to some data that you tossed into a draft of a report, but you forgot to add a reference to the source. Now that you have looked at the equation more carefully, you realize that what you have written is incomplete, and that there are parameters in the equation that you forgot to include. However, now you can remember where you saw the equation.

By taking notes, we externally record and store ideas so that we can, in principle, use or think about them in detail, later. This let's you focus on what you are doing.

It is important that you record interesting thoughts as completely as possible. Make sure that you never let anything get in the way of you writing down (or recording in a different way) an idea. You want to have as low a barrier as possible to recording your thoughts. So, whether this is audio recordings on your phone, scribbles on a notepad, or an open Word document on your computer, make sure you have some mechanism for recording information.

Principles of constructing notes

  • Atomic
  • Autonomous
  • Connected

There are three big ideas that the Zettelkasten method pushes for taking effective "permanent" notes:

  • atomic
  • autonomous
  • connectivity

  • Atomic

    One of the key principles of the Zettelkasten method is that you want to capture ideas on single note cards and that these note cards should each only contain a single idea.

    What are the issues with taking "atomic" notes?

    When I start trying to write down an idea, there ends up being a lot of concepts, information, etc. in the notes that I generate. For example, perhaps I am thinking about continuing work on developing an approximate free energy functional that satisfies self-consistency relations and is applicable to describing aggregate formation. The mathematical derivation may not nicely split up into small, individual units. They are necessarily connected.

    Why do we only want one idea per note? What is the problem with just keeping this large note that contains a bunch of ideas? I find that in writing later notes, when I return to an idea on a related topic, I end up repeating what I had said before to a large degree. Also, there is less of a tendency to go back and read my previous notes. I end up just "collecting" the ideas rather than trying to think about them to move my thoughts forward.

    We can try to fix this issue by leaving the original note in place, perhaps in a daily note, and then postpone the process of splitting the note into "atomic" notes during a later review process. The fear is that we may not be able to find these atomic notes later, but perhaps we can hedge against this by using tags related to the categories that it may be relevant to. Also, we might link the atomic note to the daily note or the original source note.

  • autonomous

    The notes should be understandable on their own. It should not require additional context for a reader to understand its meaning. It might refer to external information, but it should not depend on this external information.

    Why is this? While a note you write may make sense to you when you write it, and even a few days later, after a year or so, this may not be easy to understand.
