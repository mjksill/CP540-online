One of the things that you should try to get out of your mind is that a piece of work has an objective, intrinsic value. This is true for both theoretical and experimental work. It doesn't necessarily matter that

For example, my father was a chemist who worked for Motorola, and one of his tasks was to create a plastic to be used as packaging for semiconductor wafers that would be able to keep oxygen from coming into contact with it, thereby damaging it. However, no matter what he did, the materials that he created just kept on letting oxygen pass freely through it.

However, this material was perfect for contact lenses, where you need to let oxygen pass through the lens so that the eye can "breathe". So from the perspective of packaging for a semiconductor, this material was useless; however, from the perspective of a soft contact lens, this material was extremely valuable. Objectively, there is no intrinsic value to the material. It was all dependent on its context.

Where does value lie?

With the reader!!!

So hopefully I have convinced you that the most important aspect of professional writing is to make it valuable to the reader. Now we need to figure out how to do this. Before we can start, we need to think about what gives value to a reader.

Where does value lie? Many people think that value lies objectively in the world. What makes a piece of writing valuable? What makes a piece of work valuable?

There is no such thing as an objectively valuable piece of work, and as a corollary, there is no such thing as an objectively valuable piece of writing. The best that we can hope for is that something is collectively subjectively valuable.

Value lies in the reader.

The same piece of writing can be extremely valuable and useful to one reader and be completely meaningless and a waste of time for another. You should not try to write for the generic reader. That reader does not exist.

You need to think about difference between readers.

Write to a specific audience

Know the community

One common piece of advice that teachers often give to students about writing, is that you should target your work to be comprehensible by sixth grade students. That is, you should be writing in a style that is understandable and of interest to a broad, general audience.

This is not necessarily useful. Your writing should be targeted to a specific audience.
